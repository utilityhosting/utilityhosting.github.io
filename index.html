What’s On Your Plate?

How “healthy” meals have changed over time.

1900: Plain American fare

In 1900, doctors advised Americans to steer clear of spicy foods. A plain diet with lots of meat and dairy was thought to improve digestion.


A typical dinner might consist of chicken pudding, mashed potatoes, and creamed cabbage…

…with a side of Waldorf salad…

…and fruitcake for dessert!

1920: Jazzing up the dinner table

In the 1920s, nutrition experts endorsed “Dainty Food” – light meals composed of canned fruits and vegetables, gelatin, white sauces, mayonnaise, and egg custards. New canning technologies made packaged foods seem cleaner and more hygienic than fresh ingredients. A typical dinner might consist of chicken a la King with jellied tomato and pimento aspic and potatoes Hollandaise…

…with a side of Caesar salad…

…and Bavarian cream for dessert!

1940: Making a little go a long way

During the Great Depression and the wartime food rationing that followed, Americans tried to stretch their supply of meat with dishes like “liver loaf”. Savvy cooks economized by replacing scarce apples with cheaper, shelf-stable Ritz crackers in the national desert, apple pie. A typical dinner might consist of liver loaf with baked beans and creamed spinach…

…with a side of steamed brown bread…

…and a “mock apple pie” filled with Ritz crackers for dessert!

1950: Prosperity and preservatives

In the prosperous 1950s, advertisers promoted convenience products that brought the latest technologies into the kitchen, so that busy housewives could produce home-cooked meals with ease. Casseroles with cans of cream soup as their base were popular dishes, considered nutritious because they were enriched with vitamins and stamped with the approval of health experts. A typical dinner might consist of beef stroganoff with green bean casserole and Jello fruit salad…

…with grasshopper pie for dessert!

1970: From veal to vegetarian

Julia Child’s Mastering the Art of French Cooking inspired Americans to embrace more “sophisticated” cuisine in the 1970s. While dinner-party hosts fretted over soufflés, a health food movement emerged from the hippie subculture, championing granola, yogurt, and carrot cake (vegetables were in, but fats were not necessarily out). A typical dinner might consist of Beef bourguignon with Brussel sprouts…

…with a side of lettuce wedges with creamy dressing…

…and carrot cake with cream cheese icing for dessert!

1990: America steps on the scale

The 90s were a decade of nutritional paradoxes. Americans devoured pizza and triple-decker hamburgers, but also embraced low-fat and low-carbohydrate diets. Sounding the alarm about rising obesity levels, health experts pushed fruits, vegetables, and fish instead of red meat. Still, supermarket shelves overflowed with highly-processed “junk food”. A typical dinner might consist of grilled fish and sesame noodles…

…served with spinach salad with cranberries, goat cheese, and walnuts…

…and Dunk-a-Roos dipping cookies for dessert!
